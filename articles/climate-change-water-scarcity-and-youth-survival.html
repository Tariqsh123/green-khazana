Climate Change, Water Scarcity and Youth Survival: Are We Prepared for 2030?

By Prof Nadeem Ahmed Faraz

The intensifying climate crisis has placed water security at the heart of global survival challenges. Water and climate change are intrinsically linked: rising temperatures disrupt precipitation patterns, glacier melt, and river systems, resulting in both severe droughts and catastrophic floods. Today, over two billion people lack access to safe drinking water, and roughly half of the global population faces severe water scarcity for at least part of the year—a number projected to worsen by 2030 without decisive action.

Children and youth are disproportionately affected. According to UNICEF, 1 in 3 children worldwide—around 739 million—already live in regions exposed to high water scarcity, with South Asia bearing the highest concentration. Climate change-induced droughts and water stress compromise nutrition, health, and education outcomes, threatening educational attainment and long-term human capital development.

By 2030, global water demand is expected to outstrip supply by about 40%, intensifying competition for freshwater between households, agriculture, and industry. Without transformational policy, adaptive infrastructure, and youth-focused water stewardship programs, millions of young people face elevated risks of displacement, poverty, and conflict over resources.

To safeguard youth survival and futures, climate adaptation must prioritize water equity, sustainable management, and resilient community systems. The next decade will determine whether the world transitions from crisis to climate-water resilience.
